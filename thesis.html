Compliance is about to become the product, not the paperwork.

Why fiber-to-garment traceability turns from a nice-to-have into mandatory export infrastructure for Indian textile houses — and where a focused tool wins.

The forcing functions are already dated

Two regulatory clocks are running, and neither is speculative:

EU — Digital Product Passport. Under the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation, textiles are a priority category. Structured, verifiable product data will be a condition of sale in the EU, phased in across the 2027–2028 window. An exporter without a data spine simply cannot generate the passport a buyer's compliance team will demand. EU source
US — UFLPA. US Customs already detains shipments under the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act. The burden of proof sits on the importer, which pushes downstream to the exporter: prove your cotton's origin at the lot level, or your buyer's problem becomes your problem within a day.

The wedge: the lot-level evidence file

Large exporters will build this internally or buy an ERP module. The underserved segment is the mid-market export house — too large to hand-wave provenance, too small to fund a bespoke traceability platform. That is the wedge.

ThreadPass sells one deliverable to that segment: an audit-ready evidence file that maps a shipment back through cut-and-sew, dyeing, spinning, to the yarn purchase order. Not a sustainability narrative — a document a customs officer or a buyer's auditor accepts.

Why a focused tool beats the ERP module

  • Time to first value. An exporter can produce a compliant evidence file for one live order in days, not a six-month ERP rollout.
  • Buyer-shaped, not accountant-shaped. The output is structured for the DPP schema and the UFLPA questionnaire, because those are the two documents that actually gate revenue.
  • Standards-native. The passport format is coming; building to it now is cheaper than retrofitting an ERP later.

Build sequence

1. Readiness diagnostic (live today). The scored checklist on the home page — where does this house stand against DPP + UFLPA right now, and what are the top gaps.
2. Evidence-file generator. Structured intake of the supply chain (yarn PO → shipped garment), output a redacted sample evidence file — the deliverable prospects can hold.
3. Batch provenance records. Per-lot pages with harvest/farm/mill data and a lab-report slot, addressable by QR — the same data-traceability pattern proven on Kong Posh.
4. Buyer portal. A link an EU/US buyer opens to self-serve the passport data for a PO, closing the loop the regulation will require.

Why me

ThreadPass is that discipline pointed at a market I understand from the inside: my family is in the textile business in India.
